## Core Premise

All mainstream exercise science and nutrition research is based on **male data**. Women have been generalised to male findings. This is wrong because women's bodies operate on different hormonal frameworks across every life stage.

Women are not smaller men. They have distinct physiology from conception that diverges further at puberty, across the menstrual cycle, through perimenopause, and into post-menopause.

---

## Physiological Differences — Men vs. Women

### Muscle Morphology (Present from Birth)
- Women born with more **slow-twitch / endurance fibres** → more mitochondria, better oxygen utilisation at steady-state
- Men born with more **fast-twitch fibres** → greater anaerobic capacity, power output

### Body Composition
- Essential fat: women ~12%, men ~4–8%
- Average healthy body fat: women ~20%, men ~15%
- Women: 52% as strong as men in upper body, 66% in lower body
- With training: women can reach 70–80% of male lower-body strength

### Heart & Lungs
- Women have smaller hearts and lungs relative to body size
- Women have less haemoglobin → lower oxygen-carrying capacity
- Men have ~100% more aromatised testosterone → drives higher red blood cell count
- Result: men have greater capacity for rapid power/speed output; endurance gap is narrowing due to female fibre advantage

### The Q Angle
- Puberty causes hips to widen in females, shifting the knee-to-hip angle (Q angle)
- Q angle ~15° greater in women than men
- Creates quad dominance (over-reliance on front leg muscles vs. posterior chain)
- Centre of gravity drops; movement mechanics change completely
- By age 14, **60%+ of previously sporty girls drop out of sport** because no one explains these changes

### ACL Injury Risk
- Women are **3–4× more likely** to tear their ACL than men
- Causes: Q angle, quad dominance, weaker posterior chain, uneven force distribution through the knee
- Hormonal factor: estrogen increases ligament laxity around ovulation
- Prevention: develop posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings, calves), explosive lateral movements, single-leg jumping, proper landing mechanics

## The Hypothalamus — Key Difference in Women

The hypothalamus controls appetite, temperature, endocrine function, and energy sensing. **Women's hypothalami have more kisspeptin neurons** — highly sensitive to nutrient density.

- Baseline calorie need just to prevent endocrine dysfunction: **women = 30 cal/kg fat-free mass; men = 15 cal/kg**
- When calories drop, women's bodies sense famine → conserve energy, wind down reproductive system
- When calories are sufficient, women's systems (thyroid, hormones, cognition) all up-regulate

## Nutrition

### Fasting

| Type | Women | Men |
|------|-------|-----|
| Overnight fast (finish dinner ~6–7pm, eat within 30 min of waking) | ✅ Appropriate | ✅ Appropriate |
| Intermittent fasting (16:8) | ⚠️ Proceed carefully | ✅ Generally beneficial |
| Warrior fast (20hr fast / 4hr window) | ❌ Harmful for women | ✅ Benefits seen |

**Why warrior fasting harms women:**
- Higher resting blood glucose
- Increased fat storage
- Thyroid downregulates within 4 days
- More sympathetic (stress) drive → poor sleep, poor recovery

**Women's optimal approach:** Eat at regular intervals during the day. Stop eating 2–3 hours before bed. Eat within 30 minutes of waking to blunt natural cortisol peak.

### Fasted Training (Women)
- Fasted training burns **lean muscle mass first** (not fat) in women
- The hypothalamus panics without blood sugar → breaks down muscle as active fuel
- **Fix:** 15g protein (strength) or 15g protein + 30g carbs (cardio) before training
- This small intake is enough to signal the hypothalamus that fuel is available

### Post-Exercise Nutrition Window
- Women's metabolism returns to baseline within **30–40 minutes** post-exercise
- Men's returns in **2–18 hours** depending on intensity
- Women need **35g protein within 45 minutes** post-exercise to trigger muscle protein synthesis
- Men need only ~20g, and have a much wider time window

### Protein
- Women need higher protein than current RDA (which is based on sedentary older men)
- Protein needs increase after ovulation by ~12% (amino acids needed for uterine lining development)
- In perimenopause/menopause: women become more **anabolically resistant** → higher doses required to trigger muscle protein synthesis
- Goal: higher protein at regular intervals throughout the day

### Carbohydrates
- Women are **not good candidates for low-carb diets** for the same reasons they shouldn't fast
- After ovulation (day 14+): increased carbs needed pre and post high-intensity training
- Carbs from fruit and veg behave differently than from ultra-processed food

### Keto Diet
- **Not recommended for women (or men)**
- 70–80% fat diet destroys gut microbiome diversity
- Reduces beneficial bacteria responsible for sex hormone metabolism (estrogen/progesterone second-pass reactivation)
- Women are already metabolically flexible by nature of their fibre type — keto provides no additional benefit

### Blood Glucose
- Women's insulin sensitivity fluctuates across the menstrual cycle
- Most glucose-sensitive: **day 19–23** (high progesterone phase, post-ovulation)
- Perimenopause: chronic insulin resistance develops as hormonal confusion disrupts glucose storage signalling

## Supplements

### Creatine
- Common myth: creatine is for male bodybuilders and causes weight gain
- **Truth for women:** 3–5g/day (no loading, no carbs) saturates the body in ~3 weeks
- Benefits: gut integrity, brain health (depression/anxiety), muscle performance
- Women naturally have ~70% of male creatine stores (lower dietary intake)
- GI distress 5× more common in women during exercise — creatine improves intestinal mucosal lining
- RCTs show creatine at 3–5g/day outperforms SSRIs alone for women recovering from depression
- Loading strategy (5g × 4/day): only appropriate if targeting specific performance block or physique building

### Vitamin D3 (Not D2)
- D3 is bioavailable immediately; D2 is a storage form that must be converted
- Most people are deficient due to sunscreen use, indoor lifestyle, poor dietary sources
- Benefits: muscle function, recovery, brain health, immune function, bone density

### Omega-3
- Particularly important from late 30s onward
- Replaces some anti-inflammatory functions that estrogen handles in reproductive years
- Enhances cellular integrity during perimenopause/menopause
- Get blood test to confirm low levels before supplementing

### Iron
- Reference range "normal" for ferritin has shifted as populations get sicker
- "Normal" range now 26 for women — but for an active woman, **minimum 50, ideally 100**
- Supplement every other day (not daily) with high-bioavailable carbonyl or glycinate iron
- Hepcidin (enzyme that blocks iron absorption) is elevated:
  - 5 hours post-training in men and reproductive women
  - Up to 24 hours in late-peri and early post-menopausal women
- **Timing rule:** supplement before training or at night, away from exercise

## The Menstrual Cycle — 28-Day Framework

> Note: 21–40 day cycles are normal. The "28-day" cycle is textbook, not typical.

### Phase Overview

| Phase | Days (approx) | Hormones | Characteristics |
|-------|--------------|----------|-----------------|
| Early Follicular | 1–6 | Low estrogen, low progesterone | Bleeding; immune slightly suppressed in final days |
| Late Follicular | 6–13 | Rising estrogen | Peak performance window; resilient, glucose-sensitive |
| Ovulation | ~12–13 | Estrogen peak, LH surge | Strongest phase; estrogen aids muscle protein synthesis |
| Early Luteal | 14–23 | Progesterone rises | Fuel shift; more carbs needed; protein needs up 12% |
| Late Luteal | 23–28 | Hormones drop | Core temp up 0.5°C; poorer sleep; dial down intensity |

### Training Recommendations by Phase

**Low hormone phase (days 6–13): PUSH HARD**
- Body is highly responsive to stress
- Best time for PRs, high-intensity training, new skills
- Glucose is well-managed

**Post-ovulation (days 14–23): ADJUST**
- Add carbs before and after high-intensity sessions
- Increase protein intake by ~12%
- Reduce very high intensity; shift to tempo/endurance work
- Core temp 0.5–1°F higher → reduced heat tolerance

**Pre-bleed (days 23–28): DELOAD**
- Immune system more compromised
- Use as a deload week
- Technique work, mobility, recovery focus

### Tracking Guidance
- Use **sessional Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE)**: rate physical energy 1–10 and mental energy 1–10
- If both are low → technique and recovery day
- If physical is high, mental is low → warm up and reassess
- Track bleed pattern and cycle length: changes signal the body is under stress (over-training, under-eating, life stress)
- **55% of recreational female athletes are in a low energy availability state** — shows up as irregular or missed periods

### Anovulatory Cycles
- No ovulation, but bleeding still occurs — very common in modern women
- Causes: inadequate nutrition (common with trendy diets), stress, low energy availability
- Means progesterone is not produced → luteal phase adaptations don't occur
- Makes cycle phase tracking harder — rely on RPE and symptom tracking

## Perimenopause

**Age range:** Typically starts ~35, average menopause at age 52 → ~15–17 year transition

### What's Happening
- Ratio of estrogen to progesterone shifts erratically
- Early signs (often misdiagnosed as stress/burnout):
  - Training not working; gaining body fat despite same effort
  - Poor sleep
  - Mood changes
  - Squishy / softer body composition
- Later signs:
  - Rising LDL cholesterol (even with no prior history)
  - Rising A1C (pre-diabetes marker)
  - Significant body composition change → visceral fat (around organs)
  - Gut microbiome diversity decreases (reduced sex hormones)

### Why Visceral Fat Accumulates
- Estrogen normally has anti-inflammatory properties and helps pull free fatty acids into mitochondria
- Without estrogen: liver converts circulating free fatty acids into esterified fatty acids → stored as visceral fat
- Myokines (released by exercising skeletal muscle) can signal liver to stop this process

### Diagnosis
- No definitive blood test
- Diagnosed symptomatically; track menstrual changes (shorter/longer cycles, bleed pattern changes)

### Exercise in Perimenopause
- Can no longer rely on hormones for muscle protein synthesis or bone density
- Must use **exercise as the stimulus instead**
- Prioritise:
  - **High-intensity interval training (HIIT) / sprint intervals** — improve insulin sensitivity
  - **Plyometrics** — bone loading, power
  - **Resistance training (power-based)** — muscle, myokine release
- Less volume, more quality: short sharp sessions, not 90-min moderate-intensity sessions
- Zone 2 (moderate steady-state) is **not sufficient** for the hormonal adaptive response needed
- Frequency: resistance training 3×/week; cardio HIIT 2–4×/week

### Nutrition in Perimenopause
- Higher protein at regular intervals
- Colourful fruit and veg for gut microbiome diversity (30 different plants per week target)
- Gut microbiome diversity supports:
  - Sex hormone second-pass reactivation
  - Serotonin production
  - Vitamin synthesis
  - Lean mass preservation

### Symptom Severity Factors
- More body fat → greater hot flash (vasomotor symptom) severity
- More lean mass → less insulin resistance → milder symptoms
- Maternal history: genetic predisposition to more severe symptomology

## Menopause Hormone Therapy (MHT)

> Stacy's preferred term is **Menopause Hormone Therapy**, not HRT (Hormone Replacement Therapy)

- MHT is not about replacing hormones to "stay young"
- It is a **therapy to attenuate the rate of change** causing quality-of-life disruption
- Uses low physiologic doses — not reproductive-era levels
- **No evidence** that MHT prevents dementia
- Use alongside lifestyle interventions (exercise, nutrition), not instead of them
- Consider it a tool of last resort after exhausting exercise, sleep, and nutrition changes

## Post-Menopause

- "It gets better on the other side"
- Once through the transition with proper lifestyle interventions:
  - Joint pain reduces
  - Sleep improves
  - A new hormonal equilibrium is established
- Bone health maintenance continues to require resistance training and plyometrics

## Sleep

### Women vs. Men
- Women's sleep is disrupted by menstrual cycle in late luteal phase (progesterone raises core temp; estrogen changes melatonin pulse)
- Women's circadian rhythm is slightly **shorter** than men's
- Women's melatonin peaks earlier (~9pm average vs. ~10–11pm for men)
- Men tend toward later chronotypes

### Jet Lag
- Women do better **going east** (shorter circadian rhythm aligns better with shorter days)
- Men do better **going west**

### Sleep Optimization
- Eat dinner by 6–7pm; stop eating 2–3 hours before bed
- Eating before bed redirects parasympathetic activity to digestion, not sleep repair
- Getting into good sleep architecture improves:
  - Blood glucose control
  - Insulin response
  - Energy levels
  - Physical and mental recovery

## Cold Therapy & Sauna

### Cold Plunge / Ice Bath
- Most cold plunge research is on men
- Ice bath (0–4°C) has **different effects on women** — too much vasoconstriction, perceived as threat
- Women need **~15–16°C (55°F)** water to achieve the same physiological response men get from ice water
- At correct temperature, women can experience: improved cognition, better blood glucose, autophagy effects, sense of control

### Sauna
- Both men and women benefit from sauna
- Women vasodilate before sweating (due to thermoregulation differences) → take longer to reach the same core temperature
- Women need **longer sessions** (20–30 min vs ~10 min for men) and **more sessions over weeks** to achieve the same cardiovascular adaptations
- Sauna helps perimenopause/menopause: trains the body's temperature regulation, which reduces hot flash severity

### Heat Acclimatisation (Training for Hot Conditions)
- Men: 7 days consecutive sauna exposure is sufficient
- Women in high hormone phase (post-ovulation): 9 days consecutive works
- Women in low hormone phase: must do 5min in / out / in protocol across 9 days to acclimatise effectively

## PCOS

- Characterised by higher androgens, chronic insulin resistance, irregular cycles
- Cannot use menstrual cycle phase as a stress indicator
- Training approach: high-intensity + resistance training to manage blood glucose and insulin sensitivity
- Track readiness via heart rate variability instead of cycle phase

## Endometriosis

- Emerging evidence of bacterial/viral causes in addition to inflammation
- Practical intervention: **cold water therapy around ovulation** time
- The anti-inflammatory effect of cold water can reduce the growth of endometrial tissue outside the uterus post-ovulation
- Anti-inflammatory diet also relevant (gut microbiome diversity)

## Bone Health

- Driven by estrogen and progesterone interplay
- Peak bone mass hit around age 20
- Degrades if not maintained with **multidirectional bone stress**: jumping, resistance training
- OCP use alters estrogen/progesterone signalling → affects bone density
- Vitamin D3 essential for bone health
- Amenorrhea (loss of period) = bone health risk
- Osteopenia/osteoporosis risk is significantly higher in women than men

## Estrogen Types

| Type | Name | Role |
|------|------|------|
| E1 | Estrone | Dominant in post-menopause; weaker; produced in fat and adrenal glands |
| E2 | Estradiol | Dominant in reproductive years; most potent; protects brain, bone, heart, muscle, gut |
| E3 | Estriol | Dominant during pregnancy |

- E2 decline is the primary driver of menopause symptoms — not just "low estrogen" generically
- E2 activates Vitamin D, protects the gut lining, reduces inflammation, supports serotonin/dopamine production
- Progesterone is neuroprotective (calming) and counters excess E2 effects; ratio matters as much as absolute levels

## Heart Palpitations & Cardiovascular Changes

- ~25% of perimenopausal women experience heart palpitations
- Caused by fluctuating estrogen affecting the autonomic nervous system (not cardiac disease)
- Resting heart rate can increase by up to **16 BPM** during perimenopause
- Blood pressure becomes more variable and tends to rise as estrogen declines
- Exercise helps: resistance training and HIIT improve vascular compliance and reduce blood pressure
- Post-exercise blood pressure drop ("wobbles"): cool down properly for 5–10 min; compression garments help; avoid standing still immediately post-exercise

### HRV Tracking Caveat
- Consumer wearable HRV algorithms are calibrated on male data or young healthy women
- Menopausal hormone fluctuations cause HRV to swing independently of recovery status
- Use HRV as one signal, not the sole guide — combine with sleep quality, mood, and RPE

## Low Energy Availability (LEA)

- **Definition:** Insufficient caloric intake relative to energy expenditure from exercise
- Threshold: <30 kcal/kg fat-free mass (FFM) per day
- Women's hypothalami are extremely sensitive to LEA — reproductive, immune, thyroid, and cognitive systems all downregulate
- **55% of recreational female athletes are in LEA** — most are unaware
- Signs: irregular or absent periods, persistent fatigue, poor training adaptation, mood changes, frequent illness
- LEA is NOT the same as intentional weight loss — it can occur even in women eating "normally" who exercise a lot
- Fix: increase total food intake, prioritise pre- and post-workout fueling

## Menopause Nutrition — Macros & The Plate

### Baseline Caloric Need
- 30 kcal/kg fat-free mass minimum just to prevent endocrine dysfunction
- Active peri/menopausal women typically need 1,800–2,400+ kcal/day depending on activity

### Protein Targets
| Life Stage | Daily Target |
|------------|-------------|
| Reproductive (active) | 1.6–1.8g/kg body weight |
| Perimenopause | 1.8–2.2g/kg (anabolic resistance increases) |
| Post-menopause | 2.0–2.4g/kg |

- Distribute protein evenly — 3–4 meals with 30–40g each
- Leucine threshold: ~2.5–3g leucine per meal required to trigger MPS (muscle protein synthesis)
- Animal proteins generally hit leucine threshold more easily; plant-protein eaters must combine sources

### The Menopause Plate (per meal)
- **½ plate:** non-starchy colourful vegetables (30 different plants/week target for gut diversity)
- **¼ plate:** quality protein (meat, fish, eggs, legumes + grain combo)
- **¼ plate:** complex carbohydrates (whole grains, starchy veg, fruit)
- **Healthy fat** at every meal: olive oil, avocado, nuts, oily fish

### Cruciferous Vegetables & Estrogen Metabolism
- Cruciferous veg (broccoli, Brussels sprouts, cabbage, cauliflower, kale) contain **glucosinolates**
- Gut bacteria convert these to **DIM (diindolylmethane)** and **I3C (indole-3-carbinol)**
- DIM promotes beneficial estrogen metabolites (2-OH pathway) over inflammatory ones (16-OH and 4-OH)
- Aim: 2–3 servings cruciferous veg per day
- Food-based DIM is preferable to supplements in most cases

## Exercise Protocols — Detailed

### Sprint Interval Training (SIT)

SIT is more effective than HIIT for perimenopausal and menopausal women for hormonal adaptation and fat loss.

**Why SIT works better than Zone 2 for this population:**
- Triggers growth hormone and testosterone release (partially compensates for declining estrogen/progesterone)
- Improves insulin sensitivity acutely (each session acts like a "glucose disposal event")
- Raises EPOC (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption) significantly more than moderate cardio

**SIT Protocols:**

| Protocol | Work | Rest | Sets | Sessions/week |
|----------|------|------|------|---------------|
| Classic Tabata | 20 sec all-out | 10 sec | 8 rounds | 2–3 |
| 40/20s | 40 sec hard | 20 sec | 6–10 rounds | 2–3 |
| 30/30 | 30 sec hard | 30 sec active recovery | 8–12 rounds | 2–3 |
| Hill Repeats | 30–60 sec sprint uphill | Walk down | 6–8 repeats | 1–2 |
| Kettlebell SIT | 35 sec swings/snatch | 25 sec | 8 rounds | 2–3 |

- "All-out" = 9–10/10 perceived effort (cannot speak)
- Do NOT do SIT on consecutive days — 48 hours minimum between sessions
- Warm up 5–10 min before any SIT; cool down 5–10 min after

### Heavy Resistance Training (HRT) — Progression Framework

**Why heavy lifting is essential for menopausal women:**
- Activates satellite cells (muscle stem cells) to build new muscle tissue
- Releases IGF-1 and myokines that signal liver and organs to reduce visceral fat storage
- Directly loads bones (osteogenic stimulus) — cardio does not achieve this
- Counters anabolic resistance that increases with declining estrogen

**Three-Phase Progression:**

| Phase | Duration | Sets × Reps | Load | Rest |
|-------|----------|-------------|------|------|
| Anatomical Adaptation | 4–6 weeks | 3×15 | 50–60% 1RM | 60–90 sec |
| Hypertrophy | 6–8 weeks | 4×8–12 | 65–75% 1RM | 90–120 sec |
| Strength/Power | 6–8 weeks | 4–5×3–6 | 80–90% 1RM | 2–3 min |

- Cycle back through phases; do not stay in hypertrophy indefinitely
- **Power work:** plyometric or explosive version of each lift (jump squats, medicine ball throws, power cleans) — preserves fast-twitch fibres
- Key exercises: squats, deadlifts, hip thrusts, rows, overhead press, single-leg Romanian deadlift, lateral bounds
- **Pelvic floor note:** heavy lifting does not damage the pelvic floor when technique is correct; exhale on exertion (do not Valsalva); if symptoms occur (leaking, prolapse pressure), see a pelvic floor physio before stopping lifting

## Hydration Science

### Why Standard Hydration Advice Fails Women
- Most hydration research done on men → women have different fluid regulation (progesterone raises thirst threshold; estrogen alters aldosterone response)
- Perimenopausal women: hormonal chaos disrupts osmolality sensing
- **Plain water is often the wrong choice during exercise** — dilutes sodium, can worsen hyponatremia

### Optimal Hydration Drink (during exercise)
- Target osmolality: **200–280 mOsmol/kg** (slightly hypotonic to blood plasma of ~290)
- **Sodium-glucose co-transport** is the mechanism: glucose + sodium together are absorbed faster than either alone
- Formula for exercise >60 min: **500–750mg sodium + 20–30g carbohydrate per 500ml water**
- DIY recipe: 500ml water + ¼ tsp table salt + small amount of fruit juice or banana

### When to Drink
- **During exercise:** drink to thirst for sessions <60 min; schedule drinking every 15–20 min for sessions >60 min
- **Pre-exercise:** 500ml in the 2 hours before; if urine is pale yellow, well-hydrated
- **Post-exercise:** 1.5× fluid lost by sweat weight (weigh before and after if heat/intensity is high)
- **Avoid salt tablets** — concentrated sodium bolus without water is harmful

### Perimenopausal Hydration Changes
- Declining estrogen reduces ability to retain sodium → increased dehydration risk
- Hot flashes increase fluid losses beyond what feels apparent
- Increase sodium intake slightly (through food) during perimenopause

## Sleep — Detailed Framework

### Why Sleep Is Disrupted by Menopause
- **Progesterone** (natural sedative) declines → harder to fall and stay asleep
- **Estrogen** changes melatonin pulse timing → circadian disruption
- Hot flashes wake women during REM and slow-wave sleep → fragmented architecture
- Cortisol peaks earlier in morning → earlier waking

### The HGH Window
- Human Growth Hormone (HGH) is released primarily in the **11pm–1am** deep sleep window
- HGH drives tissue repair, fat metabolism, muscle maintenance
- Missing this window (staying up late, fragmented sleep) significantly impairs recovery and body composition
- Strategy: be asleep by 10:30–11pm consistently

### Sleep Supplements (evidence-based doses)
| Supplement | Dose | Timing | Notes |
|------------|------|--------|-------|
| Magnesium glycinate | 300–400mg | 30–60 min before bed | Reduces cortisol, supports GABA; glycinate form best tolerated |
| L-theanine | 200mg | Before bed | Promotes alpha brain waves, reduces anxiety |
| Ashwagandha | 300–600mg | Before bed | Adaptogen; reduces cortisol; KSM-66 or Sensoril extracts best studied |
| Melatonin | 0.5–1mg | 30–60 min before bed | Low dose only — high dose (5–10mg) disrupts natural production |
| Tart cherry (Montmorency) | 240ml juice or 500mg extract | Before bed | Increases melatonin precursors; anti-inflammatory |

### Sleep Hygiene Specifics for Menopausal Women
- Keep bedroom temperature **65–68°F (18–20°C)** — lower than standard advice; helps counter hot flashes
- Cooling mattress pad / moisture-wicking bedding reduces sweat-related waking
- **Yoga and meditation** have RCT-level evidence for reducing insomnia severity in menopausal women (8-week yoga programs show significant improvement)
- Avoid alcohol — disrupts sleep architecture even if it helps initiation

## Post-Exercise Recovery

### Cold Water Therapy
- Contrast bathing (alternating warm and cold) is effective for women
- **Cold water for women: 15–16°C (55–59°F)** — not ice cold (0–4°C which is designed for men)
- Cold water post-training (not immediately post-resistance session) reduces DOMS and aids recovery
- **For resistance training:** do NOT cold plunge immediately after — it blunts the muscle adaptation signal; wait 4+ hours or use for cardio recovery only

### DOMS Management
- BCAAs (particularly **leucine 3g+**) taken pre or intra-workout reduce DOMS severity
- Curcumin (500–1,000mg with piperine) reduces exercise-induced inflammation
- Tart cherry juice (240ml) post-exercise: anti-inflammatory, reduces DOMS
- Anti-inflammatory foods preferable to NSAID painkillers (which also blunt adaptation)

### Compression & Cool-Down
- Compression garments (tights, socks) during and after exercise help venous return and reduce DOMS
- **Post-exercise cool-down is non-negotiable for menopausal women** — hot flash risk increases if stopping abruptly
- Minimum 5–10 min cool-down; gradual HR reduction prevents blood pressure drop and exercise-induced hot flashes

## Symptom-Specific Guidance

### Hot Flashes During Exercise
- Exercise can trigger hot flashes via core temperature rise
- Strategy: **layer up for warm-up** (paradox — warming up gradually actually reduces severe hot flash risk)
- Wear moisture-wicking fabrics; avoid cotton
- Train in cooler environments or early morning when ambient temperature is lower
- Sauna training over weeks reduces hot flash severity (habituates thermoregulatory system)
- High-intensity training long-term reduces frequency and severity of vasomotor symptoms

### Erratic Periods & PMS Management
- During perimenopause, cycles become irregular (shorter, longer, skipped, heavier)
- **Pre-period protocol (1 week before):**
  - Magnesium 200–400mg/day (reduces cramps and mood symptoms)
  - Omega-3 1g/day (anti-inflammatory, reduces prostaglandin-driven pain)
  - Low-dose aspirin 80mg (reduces inflammatory prostaglandins) — only if no contraindications

### Vaginal Health / GSM (Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause)
- Affects ~50% of menopausal women; often under-reported due to embarrassment
- Symptoms: vaginal dryness, painful sex, recurrent UTIs, urinary urgency
- **Vaginal estrogen** (low-dose topical E2): highly effective, minimal systemic absorption, safe for most women including breast cancer survivors
- **DHEA (prasterone) vaginal insert**: converts locally to estrogen and testosterone; option for women who can't use estrogen
- **OTC options:** Replens (polycarbophil moisturiser), Revaree (hyaluronic acid insert) — helpful but less effective than hormonal options
- Regular sexual activity (or use of vaginal dilators) maintains vaginal tissue health
- **Pelvic floor physiotherapy:** treats both GSM symptoms and incontinence; should be first-line referral

### Urinary Incontinence
- ~20% of women stop exercising due to leakage — this is a medical problem, not a reason to stop training
- Types: stress (jumping, coughing), urge (sudden urge), mixed
- **See a pelvic floor physio first** before modifying training
- Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels + reverse Kegels) must be done correctly — many women over-tighten rather than strengthen
- Heavy lifting with good technique does NOT worsen incontinence when pelvic floor is functional

## Sedentary Behaviour & Daily Movement

- Exercise sessions do NOT offset sedentary time — they are separate variables
- Sitting for prolonged periods maintains elevated cortisol and reduces insulin sensitivity independently of structured exercise
- Target: **2,500–5,000 steps/day** minimum baseline; 7,000–10,000 steps/day optimal
- Breaking up sitting every 30–45 min (short walks, standing, movement snacks) has measurable benefit on glucose regulation
- "Movement snacks" (5–10 air squats, calf raises, short walk) every hour improve insulin sensitivity throughout the day

## Supplements — Full Reference (Book Version)

> This section supersedes and extends the earlier supplements section with book-specific data.

### Vitamin D3
- Target blood level: **75–100 nmol/L (30–40 ng/mL)**
- Most people need 2,000–4,000 IU/day; max safe dose: 4,000 IU/day without medical supervision
- **Get a blood test first** — over-supplementing causes toxicity
- Estrogen normally activates Vitamin D; declining estrogen in menopause worsens deficiency even at same sun exposure
- Take with fat (fat-soluble vitamin); K2 (100–200mcg) enhances calcium routing to bones

### Iron
- Peri: 18mg/day; Post-menopause: 8mg/day (no more menstrual losses)
- Supplement every other day — hepcidin suppression resets overnight; alternate-day dosing improves absorption
- Best forms: ferrous bisglycinate (glycinate), ferrous gluconate, carbonyl iron
- Pair with Vitamin C (100–200mg) to enhance non-heme iron absorption
- Avoid taking iron with calcium, coffee, tea (tannins block absorption)
- Hepcidin elevated for up to 24 hours post-exercise in peri/early post-menopausal women → take iron before training or at bedtime

### Magnesium
- RDA: 320mg/day; active women should target **320mg + 20% = ~380mg/day**
- Forms: glycinate (best absorbed, gentlest on gut), malate (energy), citrate (OK but laxative effect at higher doses), oxide (avoid — poorly absorbed)
- Benefits: sleep quality, mood/anxiety, muscle function, heart rhythm, insulin sensitivity, bone density
- Deficiency is common — most people get <70% of RDA from food
- Takes 4–6 weeks of consistent supplementation to see sleep and mood benefits

### Calcium
- **Get from food, not supplements** — calcium pills associated with increased cardiovascular event risk (arterial calcification)
- Food targets: <50 years: 1,000mg/day; 50+: 1,200mg/day
- Best food sources: dairy, fortified plant milks, sardines with bones, kale, bok choy, almonds
- Vitamin D and K2 are essential for calcium to reach bone (vs. arteries)

### Antioxidant Supplements — DO NOT TAKE
- High-dose antioxidant supplements (Vitamin C, Vitamin E, resveratrol, quercetin in supplement form) **impair training adaptations**
- Mechanism: reactive oxygen species (ROS) from exercise are the signal that triggers adaptation; antioxidants neutralise ROS and block this signal
- Multiple RCTs confirm blunted strength and endurance gains in supplementing groups
- **Get antioxidants from whole food** (fruit, veg, spices) — food-based delivery is buffered and does not impair adaptation

### DIM (Diindolylmethane)
- Natural compound derived from cruciferous vegetables
- Promotes 2-OH estrogen metabolites (beneficial, anti-estrogenic) over 16-OH and 4-OH (inflammatory, carcinogenic)
- Food-first: 2–3 servings cruciferous veg/day provides meaningful DIM
- **Supplement dose:** 100–200mg/day DIM (only if not eating sufficient cruciferous veg)
- **Caution:** do NOT take DIM if on estrogen therapy — can reduce therapeutic estrogen levels
- No benefit for post-menopausal women with very low estrogen (nothing to metabolise)

### Creatine (Updated)
- **Loading protocol:** 0.3g/kg body weight/day for 5–7 days, then 3–5g/day maintenance
- **No-loading protocol:** 3–5g/day; full saturation in ~3 weeks
- Particularly important for menopausal women: creatine stores decline with age and declining estrogen
- Benefits beyond muscle: brain health (depression, anxiety, cognition), gut mucosal integrity, energy metabolism in all cells
- Combine with resistance training for maximum muscle + metabolic benefit
- Timing: take consistently; pre-workout is slightly more effective but any time of day works

### Omega-3 (Detailed)
- Dose: **1–3g combined EPA + DHA per day**
- EPA:DHA ratio: prioritise EPA-dominant products for anti-inflammatory effects
- Best source: fatty fish 3×/week or high-quality fish oil / algal oil (vegan)
- Blood test (omega-3 index) confirms need; target index >8%
- Reduces hot flash frequency and severity in some studies
- Reduces prostaglandin-driven menstrual pain (pre-period protocol)
- Do not take high-dose (>3g/day) without medical advice — can affect clotting

## Key Principles Summary

```yaml
principles:
  - Women are not smaller men — research based on male data does not apply
  - Hormonal phase determines how a woman responds to nutrition and exercise
  - Fasted training is counterproductive for most women
  - Resistance training is non-negotiable across all life stages — heavy and progressive
  - High-intensity (SIT/HIIT) exercise is more valuable than Zone 2 for perimenopausal women
  - The gut microbiome is central to hormone metabolism in women
  - Track the menstrual cycle to understand energy, mood, and performance patterns
  - Symptoms of perimenopause are often misdiagnosed as stress or depression
  - Menopause hormone therapy is a tool, not an anti-aging solution
  - LEA (low energy availability) affects 55% of active women and silently disrupts hormones
  - Protein needs increase significantly in perimenopause/menopause due to anabolic resistance
  - Sleep before midnight matters — the HGH repair window is 11pm–1am
  - Calcium from food, not pills; antioxidant supplements block training adaptation
  - Supplements with strongest evidence: creatine (3–5g/day), vitamin D3, omega-3, magnesium (glycinate), iron (if deficient)
  - Every menopausal symptom has an evidence-based management strategy — quitting exercise is rarely the answer
```
